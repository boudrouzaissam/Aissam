The Day Oil Was Worth Less Than Nothing

By Aissam Boudrouz · Book Chapter

It was April 2020, right in the middle of the pandemic — those long, silent days when time had no shape and the streets of Morocco were half empty.
I remember scrolling through the news after iftar, sipping my mint tea, when I read something that didn’t make sense.

“The price of American crude oil has dropped below zero.”

I blinked twice. Below zero?
How could something be worth less than nothing?

Page 1 – The strange news

That night, the group chats were on fire.
Everyone was sharing the same meme:

“If oil is negative, why can’t I fill my car for free?”

Even my uncle, who doesn’t usually care about markets, called me laughing:

“So, Aissam, should I buy a barrel and store it in the garage?”

I smiled, but my economist brain started racing.
This wasn’t a joke. Something historic was happening.

For the first time since 1929, the price of American oil had gone negative.
Producers weren’t just giving oil away — they were literally paying people to take it.

So I sat down and tried to explain it, even to myself.

Page 2 – A little background (Moroccan-style)

There are three main types of oil in the world:

Brent – the European standard,

Dubai Light – for the Middle East and Asia,

West Texas Intermediate (WTI) – the American one.

Now, the American story has a twist.
The U.S. has two kinds of oil:

one heavy, hard to export,

and one light and easy to move — shale oil, or pétrole de schiste.

When Donald Trump became president, he wanted to turn the U.S. from an oil importer into an exporter.
So he poured money — more than $400 billion — into shale oil, a type that’s costly to extract but full of promise.

Everything was fine… until the pandemic arrived.

Page 3 – When the world stopped

In March 2020, planes stopped flying, factories slowed down, cars disappeared from the streets.
Suddenly, the world didn’t need as much oil anymore.

But oil production doesn’t stop as easily as traffic lights.
The pumps kept running, barrels kept filling, and storage tanks in Texas began to overflow.

Here’s the problem: storing oil is expensive.
When you have no space left and you still have barrels arriving every day, you face a strange choice — pay someone to take it away.

That’s how the price dropped below zero.

Imagine someone saying:

“Take this barrel of oil… and I’ll give you $30 for your trouble.”

Absurd, but true.

Page 4 – The hidden cause: future contracts

To understand it fully, you have to know about futures contracts — agreements to buy or sell something later at a fixed price.

In April 2020, traders who had bought oil contracts for May delivery realized they had a big problem:
the oil was coming, but they had nowhere to store it.

Storage in Texas was full, ports were closed, and no one was driving.
So they started selling those contracts as fast as they could — even if it meant losing money.

That’s how the price hit negative territory.
It wasn’t that oil lost its value forever — it was that nobody wanted to pay to keep it.

Page 5 – Back home in Morocco

And the big question everyone asked:

“So, will fuel prices drop here too?”

I laughed.
That’s the Moroccan reflex — we see global chaos and still hope to save a few dirhams at the pump.

But the answer was simple:
No, this had nothing to do with us.

The negative prices were a short-term financial phenomenon linked to American storage and contracts — not something that would travel across oceans and magically make our gas cheaper.

So I closed my laptop, finished my tea, and whispered to myself with a smile:

“Relax. The world is strange, but the hanout still sells bread at the same price.”
