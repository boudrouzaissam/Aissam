A Stroll with Inflation

By Aissam Boudrouz · Book Chapter

One morning, I got into a taxi, and before I could even fasten my seatbelt, the driver sighed and said,

"Everything's become expensive these days… we can't even afford a kilo of meat anymore. The prices just keep rising."

He wasn't angry, just tired. It's the same sentence I've been hearing from almost everyone lately — in taxis, cafés, grocery stores, even from my parents when I visit.

At home, my father often repeats, half nostalgic, half amused:

"You know, son, thirty years ago, 300 dirhams were enough to feed a family for a week. A hundred used to feel like a fortune."

I smile politely every time, but I know he's right. We're all living the same story — the same frustration that the same amount of money buys less and less each year.

The quiet conversation we all share

Prices rise quietly, like the tide. You don't notice it day by day — only when you go back to buy the same thing and realize it costs a little more.

The driver's words stayed with me that morning. When I got out of the taxi, I kept thinking: Why does it feel like our money is shrinking while everything else grows taller — rents, bills, groceries, even coffee?

That's when the economist in me woke up, not to give a lecture, but just to understand this small discomfort we all share.

The invisible erosion

If we look at it simply, what's really happening is that the purchasing power of money — its ability to buy goods and services — is quietly declining over time.

Let's call it P, our purchasing power. And let's call our wages W, and the price level — that invisible thing that makes everything more expensive — L.

In an ideal world, as prices (L) rise, wages (W) would rise at the same speed, so that P stays the same. In other words, the growth of what we earn would match the growth of what we spend.

It's like walking up an escalator that's moving down. If you walk fast enough, you stay in place. If not, you slide backward, even if you're still walking.

The ideal balance

Mathematically, economists would say: for purchasing power P to stay constant between two moments — say t₀ and t₁ — the rate of wage growth must equal the rate of price growth.

Or more simply:

W₁ / L₁ = W₀ / L₀

That's the dream scenario — when what you earn keeps pace with what things cost.

But we all know life isn't that neat. Wages don't rise every month. Markets have their own rules, and salaries are often slow to react — what economists call wage rigidity.

Meanwhile, prices? They have no patience. They go up when they want to.

So we find ourselves chasing the same balance our parents once had, watching numbers grow on paper but shrink in reality.

Walking with inflation

That day, as I walked home from the taxi ride, I thought about how inflation isn't just an economic term — it's a feeling. It's the quiet anxiety that your money is running faster than you can catch it.

But understanding it, even just a little, brings calm. Because once you see the mechanism behind it — the relationship between prices, wages, and time — it stops feeling like chaos and starts looking like a pattern.

Maybe that's what economics really gives us — not control, but clarity.

So next time someone says, "Everything's getting expensive," I might just smile and answer,

"Yes, but now we know why."

And then I'll still complain about the bread price — because understanding doesn't make it cheaper. 🍞😄
