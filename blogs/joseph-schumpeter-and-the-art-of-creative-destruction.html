Joseph Schumpeter and the Art of Creative Destruction

By Aissam Boudrouz · Book Chapter

It was one of those late nights when I couldn’t sleep, so I started scrolling through old videos on my laptop — clips about the rise and fall of famous brands.

There it was: Nokia, the company that once ruled our pockets.
Then DVD players, the kings of every living room.
And of course, Polaroid, the camera that gave us instant magic long before Instagram existed.

Three names that once meant innovation.
Three names that eventually disappeared.

As I watched, I couldn’t help but think of Joseph Schumpeter, the economist who gave us one of the most beautiful — and brutal — concepts in economic history:

Creative destruction.

Page 1 – When innovation becomes a storm

Schumpeter believed that the strength of capitalism doesn’t come from stability — it comes from its ability to destroy itself and rebuild again.

He called innovation the true engine of the economy.
Not prices, not competition, not even supply and demand — but the constant wave of new ideas that replaces the old.

Innovation, he said, is like a storm.
It arrives suddenly, breaks what existed, and creates something better in its place.

That’s what happened when smartphones appeared.
Nokia, which once had nearly half the global phone market, couldn’t keep up with Apple and Samsung.
They didn’t lose because their phones were too expensive — they lost because the game had changed.

Page 2 – When markets transform

The same story happened to DVDs when streaming appeared,
and to Polaroid cameras when digital photography took over.

Each of these industries was once the future — until someone invented a new future.

That’s the essence of Schumpeter’s theory:

Every new wave of innovation destroys something — and through that destruction, the economy grows stronger.

It’s painful for companies, workers, and investors in the short term,
but it’s also what keeps capitalism alive.

Without innovation, there’s no renewal.
Without renewal, everything stops.

Page 3 – The human side of the storm

Of course, Schumpeter also knew this “creative destruction” had a price.

When new technologies emerge, workers in old industries lose their jobs.
Factories close, skills become outdated, and fear spreads.

But he also believed this pain was temporary.
Because those same workers — once unemployed — eventually find new opportunities in the new sectors that innovation creates.

The challenge, he said, is not to stop innovation,
but to help people survive its transition.

That’s where the state comes in — not to block progress, but to make sure everyone can move with it.
To support small companies, encourage research, and protect consumers from prices that grow too fast.

Page 4 – Between destruction and hope

As I closed my laptop that night, I thought of all the “Nokias” we’ve seen —
not just companies, but ideas, habits, and even people who couldn’t adapt fast enough.

Maybe that’s what Schumpeter was trying to tell us:
that progress always has a human cost,
but it’s also what keeps the story of humanity moving forward.

Innovation destroys, yes —
but it also creates new paths, new dreams, new jobs, and new lives.

So, if one day you see your favorite brand disappear,
don’t be sad.
Maybe it’s just making space for the next revolution. 💡📱

What If Gold Were the World’s Currency? Would J.B. Say Still Defend His Law?

It was one of those quiet nights when thoughts come slowly — the kind of evening when you read an old economics book just for the pleasure of disagreeing with it.

I was revisiting Jean-Baptiste Say, one of the founding fathers of classical economics, and his famous idea known as Say’s Law:

“Every supply creates its own demand.”

A simple, elegant sentence that shaped entire generations of economists.
But as I read it again, a strange question came to me:

“If gold were still the world’s currency, would Say have dared to write that law?”

Page 1 – A law of perfect balance

Say’s Law is one of the first lessons every economics student learns.
It argues that production automatically generates the income necessary to buy what’s produced.

In other words:
when someone produces something (that’s the supply), that act creates income for workers (wages) and for business owners (profits).
Workers spend their wages on consumption — that’s demand.
Owners reinvest their profits — that’s investment, another form of demand.

So, if everyone spends and invests, the system balances itself.
Supply creates its own demand.
And, in theory, crises are impossible.

A beautiful law — but one that works only under perfect conditions.

Page 2 – When people stop spending

Now, let’s imagine that one worker decides not to spend his income.
He keeps the money instead of buying goods.

Or imagine that the business owner — instead of investing — chooses to save his profits, waiting for “a better time.”

In both cases, money stops moving.
It’s like placing a stick in the wheel of a bicycle — the whole system slows down and eventually stops.

Say knew this, of course.
That’s why his law depends on two big assumptions.

Page 3 – The two fragile assumptions

First, money must be neutral.
That means it should have no purpose other than being a unit of value and a medium of exchange.
People should use it only to buy and sell, not to hoard or speculate.

Second, savings must equal investment — the classic assumption that everything saved eventually becomes invested somewhere else.
In mathematical terms: S = I.

If both conditions hold, then yes — every act of production automatically creates an equal act of demand.
The economy becomes a perfect circle, always in motion.

But the real world doesn’t always play by such clean rules.

Page 4 – And what if money were gold?

Now, let’s go back to the question that started all this.
What if, instead of paper and digital money, the world still used gold as its currency?

Could Say’s Law survive?

I don’t think so.

Because gold isn’t “neutral.”
It’s not just a number on a screen; it’s a treasure.
When fear rises — during wars, crises, or pandemics — people don’t spend gold; they hide it.

And the moment people start storing their money instead of using it, Say’s beautiful circle breaks.
Production no longer guarantees demand.
The economy stops breathing.

Page 5 – The human factor

That’s why Say’s Law, like many elegant economic theories, depends less on logic and more on trust.
It assumes that people will always behave rationally,
that they will always spend, invest, and believe in the system.

But history — and human nature — say otherwise.

When people are afraid, they hold on to what shines.
They hide their gold, and with it, the movement of the economy slows down.

So yes, if gold were the world’s money, I doubt Jean-Baptiste Say would have written his famous law.
Because gold may glitter,
but it doesn’t circulate easily —
and an economy that doesn’t circulate, doesn’t live. ✨
