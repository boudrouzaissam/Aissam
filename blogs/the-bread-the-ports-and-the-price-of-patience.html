Supply Inflation · COVID Economy

The Bread, the Ports, and the Price of Patience

By Aissam Boudrouz · Book Chapter

It was around 2021, a strange time in Morocco — right after the long months of lockdowns, when life was slowly coming back to normal.
Markets were opening again, cafés were buzzing, and people finally began to breathe after what felt like years indoors.

But something else came back too — the complaints.
You could hear them everywhere: in taxis, at the market, at home.

“The prices are crazy these days!”
“Even potatoes are expensive now.”
“It’s all politics, I’m telling you!”

Everyone had their own explanation — some blamed the government, others blamed “les fils de…” (you know the rest 😅).
But deep down, I knew the story was much bigger than one man or one policy.

Page 1 – When the prices climb faster than us

I remember sitting at home one evening, watching the news. Prices of basic goods — oil, sugar, flour — were all rising.
It felt sudden, but as an economist, I knew it never happens overnight. Prices move like waves: quietly at first, then all at once.

Inflation, simply put, is the steady rise in prices over time. But this time, it wasn’t the usual demand inflation (when people buy too much) — it was something else: supply inflation.

In other words, prices were rising not because we were buying too much, but because there wasn’t enough to buy.

Page 2 – The bread village

Let me explain it the Moroccan way.

Imagine you live in a small village, and there’s only one hanout — one small store.
You’re the only customer who buys bread there, about ten loaves a day.
So the shopkeeper always brings eleven, just in case.

One day, you travel for a while. The shopkeeper stops bringing bread because you’re gone.
Then you come back suddenly — hungry, craving your usual ten loaves — but he doesn’t have any left.

Now you have to go to the next village to get bread. That means transport costs, extra time, maybe even paying a delivery boy.
The result? Bread gets more expensive for you.

That’s supply inflation.
Not because the bread changed, but because the conditions around it did.

Page 3 – The world’s “hanout”

The same thing was happening globally.
After the first COVID waves, factories reopened and people started buying again — cars, clothes, electronics, everything.
But the global “shopkeeper,” also known as East Asia, wasn’t ready yet.

Ports were blocked, ships delayed, containers stuck for weeks.
Half of the world’s goods come from that region — so when those ports closed, it was like the only hanout in the world shutting its door.

Demand came back fast, but supply was crawling behind.
Result: prices rose everywhere — from bread and oil to phones and cars.

Page 4 – A passing cloud

So yes, prices were rising, but not because of “bad guys” or conspiracies.
It was just the economy trying to breathe again after a storm — like a crowded market reopening after a long rain.

I remember telling a friend at that time:

“This kind of inflation is like a passing cloud. Once supply catches up with demand, the sky clears again.”

Because in our small village example, once you’re back home for good, the shopkeeper will start ordering bread again.
Slowly, things will balance out.

That’s the rhythm of the market — chaotic at first, then calm again.

Inflation, too, has its moods.
Sometimes it’s a monster; sometimes, just a hungry customer waiting for the baker to open the oven. 🍞
