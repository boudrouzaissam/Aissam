Potential GDP and the Hidden Gap of Human Skills

By Aissam Boudrouz · Everyday Economics

Potential GDP is often defined as the level of output an economy can produce when its resources are used efficiently, without creating excessive inflationary pressure.

In macroeconomics, we usually think about capital, labor, technology, and productivity. But there is another hidden dimension: the underuse of human skills.

An economy may have people employed, but not necessarily employed in positions that fully use their abilities. This creates a silent productivity gap.

The hidden output gap

When skilled workers occupy jobs below their qualification level, the economy loses part of its productive potential. The official unemployment rate may look acceptable, but the economy still operates below what it could achieve.

This is not only a personal problem. It is a macroeconomic issue because unused skills mean lower innovation, lower productivity, weaker income growth, and less efficient allocation of human capital.

Why it matters

Potential GDP should not only be understood through machines, factories, and hours worked. It should also reflect whether the economy is using the right skills in the right places.

A society that fails to mobilize its human capital fully may underestimate its true potential and misread the nature of its economic weaknesses.
