The Dutch Disease: When Wealth Becomes a Curse

By Aissam Boudrouz · Book Chapter

It all started with a discovery — not of gold, but of gas.

In the early 1960s, in the quiet northern city of Groningen, the Netherlands struck what seemed like pure luck: vast underground reserves of natural gas. Overnight, the country became one of the world's major energy exporters. The government rejoiced, the newspapers celebrated, and the future looked brighter than ever.

But only a few years later, something strange happened. The Dutch economy — instead of booming — began to slow down. Factories closed, unemployment rose, and industrial exports fell. There was no global crisis, no war, no political turmoil.

So what happened?

Economists called it the Dutch Disease — the paradox of a nation becoming weaker after finding new wealth.

The hidden mechanism of prosperity

Here's how it works.

When a country discovers valuable natural resources — oil, gas, or minerals — the new exports bring in massive foreign currency inflows. That sudden success pushes up the value of the national currency.

At first, it sounds great — a stronger currency, a richer country. But as the exchange rate rises, it becomes more expensive to produce and export other goods, like manufactured products or agricultural items.

Investors, looking for lower costs, start moving their money elsewhere. Factories shut down, industries shrink, and workers lose their jobs.

Soon, the economy becomes dangerously dependent on one sector — the extractive one — which employs very few people.

What began as a blessing slowly turns into an illness.

From the North Sea to the heart of Africa

The Dutch Disease didn't stop in Europe. It quietly spread, like an invisible pattern, across the world.

Look at Africa today: countries rich in oil, gas, and minerals often remain among the poorest on Earth.

Take coltan, for example — a rare mineral used in almost every smartphone and laptop. Nearly 70% of the world's supply comes from African soil, especially the Democratic Republic of Congo.

Or look at Nigeria, Angola, Algeria, Libya, and Egypt — nations with vast oil and gas reserves. Yet many of them still struggle with poverty, unemployment, and political instability.

The paradox persists: the richer the ground, the poorer the people.

The economic trap

When resource wealth floods a country, it often reshapes its economy around extraction. The currency appreciates, manufacturing becomes uncompetitive, and imports replace domestic production.

Governments start using their resource revenues to buy foreign goods and subsidize consumption — but that creates a fragile balance, vulnerable to any drop in global prices.

Once the price of oil or gas falls, the entire economy trembles. And when jobs disappear, anger rises.

Some countries, like Qatar and the United Arab Emirates, saw the trap coming and diversified early — investing in tourism, finance, and technology.

Others, like Venezuela, fell deep into the cycle: nationalization, over-reliance, inflation, and collapse.

The deeper lesson

The Dutch Disease isn't really about resources. It's about dependence — on one thing, one price, one dream.

An economy that forgets to diversify its skills, industries, and ideas will always be fragile, no matter how much treasure lies beneath its soil.

In the end, true wealth isn't what you find underground — it's what you build above it. 🌍💡
