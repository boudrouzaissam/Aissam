The Price of Comfort

By Aissam Boudrouz · Everyday Economics

Comfort has value. We pay for softer seats, faster services, warmer spaces, better locations, and easier experiences.

But economics teaches us that more comfort does not always produce the same increase in satisfaction.

Diminishing returns

The first improvement in comfort may feel important. The second may still be pleasant. But after a certain point, each additional improvement brings less satisfaction than the previous one.

This is the idea of diminishing marginal utility.

The lesson

The price of comfort is not only financial. It can also be psychological. Sometimes we chase more comfort without asking whether it still increases our well-being.

Economics reminds us that value is not only about having more. It is about understanding when more stops meaning better.
