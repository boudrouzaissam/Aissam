The Economics of Waiting: Why Time Has a Price

By Aissam Boudrouz · Everyday Economics

Waiting feels passive, but economically it is active. When we wait, we spend time. And time has a price.

Whether we wait in a queue, for a reply, for a service, for an opportunity, or for a decision, we are giving up alternative uses of our time.

Waiting as an economic cost

The cost of waiting is not always visible. It may not appear on a receipt. But it exists in the form of lost productivity, stress, uncertainty, or delayed satisfaction.

This is why faster services, priority access, delivery platforms, and appointment systems have economic value. They reduce the cost of waiting.

The lesson

Waiting teaches us that time is not empty. It is a resource. And like all scarce resources, it has an opportunity cost.

Sometimes the real price of something is not what we pay in money, but what we lose while waiting for it.
