Gary Becker: When a Parking Decision Became a Nobel Idea

By Aissam Boudrouz · Book Chapter

It was sometime in the 1960s at Columbia University in New York.
A young professor named Gary Becker was running late for an oral exam with one of his students.
He reached the campus parking lot only to find a long line of cars waiting for tickets.

If he stayed in line, he’d be late.
If he parked on the street, he might get fined.

So, like any economist, he started doing what economists do best — calculating.

He thought:

“If I wait, I’ll miss the exam and lose time.”
“If I park illegally, I might get a ticket — but maybe I won’t.”

Then he looked around. Many other cars were parked illegally too.
The probability of getting fined was small.
So, comparing the cost of being late to the expected cost of a ticket, Becker made his decision.

He left the car on the street, smiled, and said to himself:

“I just committed a crime.”

But this small moment — half guilt, half curiosity — was the beginning of an idea that would one day change economics forever.

Page 1 – A different way to see crime

Later, Becker couldn’t stop thinking about that incident.
He began reading about crime — its causes, its psychology, its moral roots.

Most studies at the time said the same thing:

“Crime is a social illness. Criminals are irrational or mentally unstable.”

But Becker disagreed.
He had just committed a small “crime” himself, and it wasn’t out of madness — it was a rational calculation.

So he wrote a book that would become a classic:
Crime and Punishment: An Economic Approach.

His argument was revolutionary.
He showed that crime isn’t always emotional or pathological — it can be the result of economic reasoning.
People weigh the potential benefits of an action against its potential costs, just like any other decision in life.

Even breaking the law can be — in a strange way — rational.

Page 2 – Expanding the economic lens

Thirty-two years later, Becker went even further.
He expanded his research to explore how discrimination, family relationships, and social behavior influence economic outcomes.

He started to see economics not just as numbers and markets, but as a way to understand human life itself.

He called it:

“The Economic Way of Looking at Life.”

And with that, he won the Nobel Prize in Economics.

Page 3 – The everyday genius

What made Becker’s work so powerful wasn’t that it was complicated — it was that it was obvious.

He showed that ordinary people — all of us — make economic decisions every day, whether we realize it or not.
When we decide to skip the bus and walk, when we delay buying something, when we take a small risk — we are all doing a kind of mental cost-benefit analysis.

He made economics human again.

Page 4 – The simple thought behind a revolution

That day in the 1960s, Becker wasn’t thinking about Nobel Prizes or new theories.
He was just a man in a hurry, deciding between a parking fine and being late.

But in that small moment, he discovered something profound:

even behind our smallest choices,
there’s a quiet logic —
a mix of reason, risk, and hope.

Simple ideas, born from daily life.
That’s where the greatest economics often begins. 🚗💭
